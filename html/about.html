ABOUT

Human trafficking is a global phenomena. Over 40 million people are trafficked each year and trafficking is embedded within the global economy, as trafficked laborers create products that are shipped into our homes and trafficked sex workers work in cities around the world. The UN has taken up goals to prevent and monitor trafficking yet little is still understood about trafficking, as data collection, monitoring, and enforcement remain sporadic. This has not stopped countries from exploiting fears about trafficking, using the public’s lack of awareness of trafficking to make false claims about trafficking as a way to justify harsh border restrictions.

We used data from the International Organization of Migration, which contains information about people trafficked between 2002 and 2018 to create a website that engages the general audience and creates awareness about the topic of human trafficking, which is constantly an invisible type of migration. The visualizations are intended to be used as data exploration tools, both the varieties of ways trafficking operates--within and across countries, and involving both rich and poor countries--and to display how much data is still missing even from public data-collection tools. This will allow the general public, NGOs and other institutions to explore in detail the data and help them advocate for policies to prevent trafficking.

VISUALIZATIONS

The colors and fonts used intend to create a sober and dramatic aesthetic, to convey the seriousness of this topic, while at the same time artistic, to attract and engage a general audience that might not be familiar with human trafficking.

We wanted to engage the visitor from their first interaction with the website, which is why we start with a survey that the visitors can interact with, and show in real time what others believe to be the total number of people trafficked. By showing these results, we believe that the public will compare to others, but also learn how the general perception of the total number of people trafficked is actually lower than the real magnitude.

The two visualizations that we created are a dot map, to explore the demographics and characteristics of the people trafficked between 2002 and 2018, and a Sankey diagram, to explore the flows of people between countries.

In the dot map, every dot represents a person, from a total of XXXX. The data can be symbolized with different colors and ordered by different categories. This maintains the granularity of the data but allows to shows general patterns of the dataset. On the other hand, the Sankey Diagram shows the relationship between people trafficked and the location they have been trafficked from and to. This helps identify which countries are the ones where most people have been trafficked, the flow between countries, and where there is more internal (trafficked inside their own country) or external (trafficked to outside of their country) human trafficking.

LIMITATIONS

As we sorted through and picked apart the dataset from the International Organization for Migration we realized that one of the biggest obstacles in the way of combating human trafficking is its lack of reliable and comprehensive data. The availability of data for each country depends heavily on the presence of IOM programming and their government’s response rating and policies against human trafficking. The paradox of this is that human trafficking data is most needed in places with the least amount of governmental supervision and international organization presence since that is most likely where it is the most invisible and the most prevalent. However, those places are also the countries with the least amount of available data in the database. As a result, places that appear to have low or high levels of trafficking in our visualization may just be those places that collect the least or most data on trafficking, or do the best job of detecting and monitoring trafficking.

Using the Global Slavery Index (hyperlink) as a reference for vulnerability to modern day slavery and governmental response rating, we find that of the top 25 most vulnerable countries, with scores ranging between 65.6 and 100), the IOM only has data on 3 and the aggregate data makes up only .84% of the entire database. Our main limitation in creating a useful visualization was the lack of comprehensive and representative data. The data we used to derive our information from validates the name of our project: Human Trafficking is a shadow - impossible to pinpoint and catch, and at many times invisible.

ANTI-TRAFFICKING RESOURCES

If you suspect you’ve witnessed someone being trafficked, you should call the National Human Trafficking Hotline at 1-888-373-7888, text the hotline at 233733, or chat with the hotline on the hotlines website.

If you want to learn more about human trafficking or become more involved in anti-trafficking work, you can visit the following resources and organizations:

  • The Polaris Project, an anti-human trafficking organization that provides services to trafficked persons and conducts anti-trafficking advocacy and research.
  • The International Labour Organization, whose Forced Labour and Forced Marriage report provides the global estimates of trafficking.
  • The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime, whose Global Report on Trafficking in Persons provides additional data on trafficking as well as region-level information on trafficking flows, and whose human trafficking indicators provide ways to recognize trafficking when you see it.
  • The Global Slavery Index, which visualizes trafficking vulnerability and tracks government responsiveness to human trafficking.
  • The Counter Trafficking Data Collaborative, a hub of data on trafficking from different countries.

TEAM

We are Cindy Si, Dani Chong, and Dan Powers, a team of three students at MIT who worked on this project for our Big Data Visualization and Society Class for our professor, Sarah Williams. Our assignment was to create a visualization to show information about a topic related to migration. We chose human trafficking because although it is a violation of human rights and is intertwined with the global economy, there is a lack of awareness about the topic and its real magnitude.

The inspiration for our visualizations came from Kazuki Ogiwara’s sankey diagram and map displaying information on trafficking, and from the project Conflict Urbanism: Colombia created by the Center for Spatial Research at Columbia University, and the Masters in Peace Building at the Universidad de Los Andes.
